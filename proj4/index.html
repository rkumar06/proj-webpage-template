Part 2. Simulation via Numerical Integration

With the lower ks, we see that the cloth sags more which illustrates how gravity increases with a smaller ks value. In comparison, with a higher ks, the cloth sags less which shows a smaller force of gravity on the cloth.


With the lower density, we see that the cloth sags less which illustrates how the force of weight decreases with less density since it is a lighter material now. In comparison, with a higher density, the cloth sags more which shows a higher force of weight on the cloth since the material itself is heavier.


Damping doesn't effect the final result of the cloth hanging; however it does affect how fast it falls down. This is harder to see in an image since the main aspect is the movement, but in the image with less damping, more ripples can be seen due to the faster movement of falling and therefore more internal springs have been set off. In comparison, with an increase damping, the cloth is falling a lot slower and smoother as if it is lightly being placed down.



Part 3. Handling collisions with other objects

As observed from part 2, the ks value affects how the gravity affects the objects in the scene. With the smaller ks value of 500, the force with gravity increases as the cloth sags more which is illustrated in the image with ks = 500. In comparison with the ks = 50,000, the force of weight from the gravity is less so it seems more flowy and doesn't sag as much. The scene with ks = 5,000 meets these situations in the middle where is sags from gravity but not too much or too little in comparison to the others.



Part 4. Handling self-collisions


We've discovered that a low ks corresponds to a stronger force of weight from a higher acceleration of gravity. This is illustrated when it falls since there are more self-collisions and faster self-collisions that occur which is illustrated in the extra bounces in the image.


In comparison, with a higher ks, we've discovered that it corresponds to a weaker force of weight from a lower acceleration of gravity. This is illustrated when it falls since there are less self-collisions at a slower rate which is illustrated with less bounces in the cloth. It appears smoother as it falls.


The low density cloth falls similarly to a high ks scene since the cloth is lighter so it can fall more smoothly. We can see this in the image as there are less bounces in the cloth.


On the flip side, a high density cloth is heavier and is similar to a low ks situation where there are more self-collisions which can be seen with the increase of bounces in the cloth as it falls.


Part 5. Shaders

Shader programs are isolated but run in parallel. They each are accountable for a part of the graphics pipeline. They go from inputs to outputs. In this project we used vertex and fragment shaders. Vertex shaders transform model-space vertices into world space. They handle the vertex processing stage of the rasterization pipeline. Fragment shaders are responsible for creating lighting effects and applying colors. They work together to create complicated graphics such as the following.

Blinn-Phong runs seperate calculations for different parts of an image based on how the lighting and image should look at that point. It relies on three variables for lighting: ambient, diffuse, and specular. These parts make up shininess, bouncing light, and constant light respectively.
